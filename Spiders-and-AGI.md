# A Jumping Spider Is More Intelligent Than Your AGI Framework

**Why Google DeepMind's Cognitive Taxonomy Repeats Mistakes We Identified Thirty Years Ago**

*Jaime F. Moraga, PhD — Applied AI Leader, Accenture/DataStories*

---

Google DeepMind recently published "Measuring Progress Toward AGI: A Cognitive Taxonomy," proposing ten cognitive faculties — perception, reasoning, memory, metacognition, and so on — as the definitive scorecard for artificial general intelligence. The framework is rigorous within its own boundaries. It is also profoundly incomplete — and the gaps it exhibits are not new. Many of them were already well-understood in the mid-1990s.

In my 1996 thesis on hybrid systems for AI at the Universidad de Santiago de Chile, I addressed several of these same questions: the need for embodied intelligence and external agency, the limitations of *g* and even of Gardner's multiple intelligences framework, and the biological foundations of cognition — particularly Maturana and Varela's conception of living beings as autopoietic cognitive systems. These were not exotic ideas then. They were central to the discourse in cognitive science, cybernetics, and the Chilean school of biology of cognition. That a major AI lab's flagship framework for measuring general intelligence still hasn't absorbed them three decades later is worth examining.

**The spider test**

Consider the jumping spider. With roughly 300,000 neurons, a salticid navigates in four dimensions — three spatial plus time — planning multi-step detour routes, continuously self-correcting toward its goal as conditions change. It categorizes prey types to select different hunting strategies, learns from single encounters, and accomplishes all of this with depth perception from a body smaller than your fingernail. It does none of this through "reasoning" or "metacognition" as DeepMind defines them. Yet no biologist would call this creature unintelligent.

DeepMind's framework cannot even score it. If your framework for measuring *general* intelligence cannot account for one of the most successful cognitive architectures in the animal kingdom, it is not measuring general intelligence. It is measuring how closely a system approximates one particular model of human cognition.

**Intelligence is not cognition — it is viability**

The paper treats intelligence as a collection of cognitive capacities, essentially a psychometric decomposition. But Maturana and Varela showed us decades ago that cognition is not information processing — it is the process by which a living system maintains its organization in interaction with its environment. Intelligence, in this view, is inseparable from autopoiesis: the capacity of a system to continuously produce and maintain itself, to generate its own goals, and to adapt its coupling with its environment.

Agency and autonomous goal-generation — arguably the most fundamental features of intelligent behavior across every biological lineage — are entirely absent from DeepMind's taxonomy. A system that scores in the 99th percentile on all ten faculties but cannot autonomously decide what matters is not generally intelligent. It is an extraordinarily sophisticated tool.

**The anthropomorphism trap**

There is a perverse asymmetry in how we attribute intelligence. We readily project mental models, emotions, and human-like understanding onto systems that are far simpler than they appear — Weizenbaum demonstrated this with ELIZA in 1966, and we see it repeated today every time someone describes a chatbot as "understanding" or "feeling." Interactive toys, digital pets, and large language models all trigger the same anthropomorphic reflex. Yet we simultaneously reject the intelligence of narrow AI systems once we understand their mechanisms. Once you explain how ELIZA works, people immediately retract their attribution of intelligence. The magic evaporates when the box is opened.

This asymmetry distorts the entire AGI measurement enterprise. A framework built around human cognitive faculties will systematically overvalue systems that *look* human-like (fluent language, plausible reasoning patterns) and undervalue systems that achieve intelligent outcomes through alien architectures. It measures mimicry, not intelligence.

**One tradition's lens is not a universal ruler**

The authors present their framework as drawn from "decades of research in psychology, neuroscience, and cognitive science." This is accurate but incomplete — it is a specific slice of that research: the Western psychometric tradition rooted in factorial models of cognition. Gardner's multiple intelligences, Sternberg's triarchic theory, emotional intelligence research, cross-cultural intelligence studies, and the entire tradition of enactivist and embodied cognition have spent decades showing that the factorial decomposition keeps missing things that matter for real-world functioning. Even Gardner's framework, which was a genuine advance over *g*, remains limited by its focus on categorizing *human* faculties rather than addressing what intelligence fundamentally is.

By treating one tradition's categories as comprehensive, the paper quietly inherits all its biases.

**The selection bias we're not talking about**

There is a further problem that deserves scrutiny: the ten faculties selected are not just a scientific choice. They are the faculties that map most readily onto capabilities that can be benchmarked, productized, and funded. Reasoning, memory, perception, generation — these are the building blocks of systems that VCs can evaluate and companies can sell. Embodied intelligence, autopoietic agency, environmental coupling, genuine social understanding — these are harder to package into a product roadmap.

This is the same selection bias visible in the now-familiar aspiration of building an AI system that can "autonomously run a billion-dollar company." That framing reveals what much of the AGI conversation actually is: a discussion about economic value capture dressed in the language of science. A framework for general intelligence that happens to measure exactly the capabilities most relevant to near-term commercial AI products is not wrong, but it is not neutral either.

The Kaggle hackathon with $200,000 in prizes completes the picture — a mechanism for community adoption of one lab's framing, reinforced by incentives. Whoever defines the ruler defines what "progress" means. Anyone who has studied how institutions capture and ossify original insights should recognize the pattern.

**What would a better framework look like?**

A serious framework for general intelligence would need to account for at least the following:

- **Embodiment and environmental coupling** — intelligence as situated action, not disembodied computation.
- **Autopoietic agency** — the capacity to generate and revise one's own goals, not merely optimize for externally specified objectives.
- **Biological generality** — a framework that can score a jumping spider, an octopus, a human child, and an AI system on the same continuum.
- **Reconciliation against reality** — validation that cognitive profiles actually predict real-world outcomes, measured through operational metrics, not self-referential benchmarks.
- **Resistance to anthropomorphic bias** — evaluation methods that distinguish genuine capability from surface mimicry.
- **Independence from commercial incentives** — measurement that isn't shaped by what's fundable.

None of this is new. The conceptual tools have been available since at least the mid-1990s, and most of them far longer. The question is whether the field will use them — or keep rediscovering the same blind spots every decade.
